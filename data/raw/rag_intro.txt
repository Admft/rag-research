Retrieval-Augmented Generation, often called RAG, is a technique that combines information retrieval with text generation.

A RAG system usually has two major parts. First, it retrieves relevant documents or passages from a knowledge source. Second, it gives those retrieved passages to a language model so the model can answer using external information.

RAG is useful when a language model does not know specific facts, private documents, recent information, or domain-specific material.

A basic RAG pipeline includes document loading, chunking, embedding, indexing, retrieval, and answer generation.

Chunking is the process of splitting long documents into smaller pieces. Chunk size and chunk overlap can strongly affect retrieval quality.

Dense retrieval uses embedding vectors to find semantically similar text. BM25 uses keyword matching. Hybrid retrieval combines dense retrieval and keyword retrieval.

Common RAG evaluation metrics include Recall@k, Precision@k, Mean Reciprocal Rank, faithfulness, answer correctness, latency, and citation accuracy.

RAG systems can fail when retrieval misses the right context, when retrieved chunks conflict, when chunks are too small, when chunks are too large, or when the generator ignores the retrieved context.
